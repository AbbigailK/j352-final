The Pressure for Perfection

By: Abbigail Klein

December 11, 2019

(Image from everythingmom.com)

Conquiring the stigma behind body dysmorphia among college women

In today’s technological society, young women not only struggle with the pressure to appear flawless but also with the idea that this social burden can lead to a mental disorder. Navigating these evolving feelings of self-expression, the University of Maryland is not unlike other college campuses. Surveys were collected from women affiliated with sororities and female student athletes at the University of Maryland, College Park. Questions were provided in the survey, intending to elicit a more in-depth thought analysis in regard to body images. Therefore promoting more of a positive light around body dysmorphia.

“One of most important influences on body dissatisfaction and appearance focus in adolescent girls is how appearance focused their peers are.”
- Heather R. Gallivan of Park Niccollet Melrose Center.

Although this survey can gain recognition and similarities from campuses elsewhere, focusing on one particular campus could be the stimulus needed to effectuate a changing dynamic relating to body dysmorphia.

(Image from Charlotte Astrid)

Body dysmorphia a mental disorder that dramatically skews one's perception of him or herself, is particularly common among young women — though it is not widely understood. The disorder can lead to anxiety or other compulsions, and many are unwilling to seek guidance or even admit a problem. 

Each sample group had similar questions. The anonymous study also included short answer responses aiming to receive the most honest and thorough feedback. The goal was to focus directly on the female’s self-image and goals, while also gaining perspective on the greater picture, whether in a sorority or on an athletic team.

“Before I joined a sorority, I was familiar with body dysmorphia, but after living in my sorority house I have realized how many girls struggle with the image they see in the mirror. It’s honestly really sad.”
- Audrey Decker, a member of the Delta Delta Delta chapter at UMD. 

Eight different sororities on campus were surveyed. Out of the 65 responses, only nine women are comfortable with their current weight and have never been upset with their body shape. The 56 others aspire to lose weight, feel more comfortable with their bodies and have been emotionally affected by their perceived body shape. Although losing weight can be healthy, several cases exhibited a drastic weight loss, which could be detrimental both physically and emotionally.

Weight is a controversial topic as women today are hesitant about answering truthfully if it were to come up in conversation. Both surveys asked women to put their actual weight and their ideal weight. Many women’s current weight was greater than what they envisioned as their ideal weight. However, several believed that their ideal weight was in fact their current weight.

In addition to diet, exercise is part of the whole process to attaining self-goals. Exercise regimens have been a factor contributing to confidence and how women portray their image. In a sorority, over 35% of women stated that they work out between 2-4 hours a week, while 7% of women work out every day. 

Social media has become a part of people’s everyday lives, a platform used by all. Those who responded to the survey all responded yes to using Instagram. However, 2% of women in sororities do not use Snapchat, while 3% of them do not use Facebook, both popular applications.

(Image from Not A Diet)

A contributing factor to body imaging is based upon how bodies, in particular the presence of perceived flaws or the lack of, are depicted across different social media platforms.

Dr. Jasmine Fardouly Macquarie University’s Centre for Emotional Health has recognized trends and concerns on social media. She finds that "people tend to curate their social media feeds in order to present the best version of themselves on social media (Zhao, Grasmuck, & Martin, 2008), some tend to post only the most attractive images on these sites” (Fardouly). 

Although both members of a sorority and athletes on a sports team lead different lives, it is clear the effect social media has on body imaging. Unfortunately, using technological platforms as a means of motivation ultimately leads to social pressures and emotional distress. The chart below shows the responses to how girls, both in a sorority and on an athletic team, emotionally feel towards the images they view on Instagram, Snapchat, and Facebook. Jealous, depressed/sad, angry, other feelings and all of the above were the options provided. Most women in a sorority and on a sports team felt jealousy based off of the content they saw on social media. 

Based on the comments, affiliating with a sorority or being on a sports team while in college, has adversely impacted people's attitude towards their bodies. Despite these negative trends, some individuals have begun deleting their social media accounts in order to focus more on themselves, rather than someone else. Additionally, Dr. Fardouly also found that “there is a link between spending more time on social media platforms or engaging with more appearance-related content (e.g., images) on social media and greater body image concerns and disordered eating among young men and women” (Fardouly).

(Image from missmuslim.nyc)

Within the sorority questionnaire, a majority of the women admitted to judging people on social media. Answers of what the judgement included were “the way they look and what experiences they are having”, “appearance, style, wealth”, “if they are pretty and skinny”, and “how skinny their stomach is.”

Interestingly, all the female athletes that responded admitted to judging others on social media. Their reasoning is as follows, “clothes, hair, makeup, body”, “type of posts and looks”, and “appearance and how they show themselves.”

Very similar to the viewpoints of those in a sorority, each of them focuses on appearance. A majority of the women acknowledged that they use social media to gain inspiration about how to look.

A woman in Kappa Alpha Theta said that social media is more of a reminder rather than a tool to attain inspiration, while a member of the Alpha Phi chapter relies on Instagram to influence her outfit choices.

However, women today have varied opinions about how they can help conquer body imaging. Those in sororities and on sports teams were positive, in that they all felt they could provide insight and help to those that may be struggling.

Sorority Responses
  • By reminding them they are beautiful and to continue eating a balanced diet
  • Emotional support, encouragement of healthier behaviors/eating habits
  • Talk to them about their habits and encourage them to see their beauty!
  • Tell them it’s okay to eat a cheeseburger!
  • Telling people they look beautiful when they do
Athlete Responses
  • I try to get them to understand that there is no set ideal, body shape/weight.
  • I think it’s just important to boost other people up. There is no reason to feed into making other people self-conscious or concerned with how they look.
  • Recommending they seek help and trying to explain to them the importance of food as fuel not.

People’s experiences and interactions with others have become the foundation for solutions.
